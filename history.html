THE HISTORY OF CERAMICS

The Origin and Legacy of Ceramics

Ceramics, one of humanity's earliest crafts, encompasses wisdom and culture from thousands of years. The clay, fired and hardened, proved to be highly durable and practical for the people of the Neolithic Age. Thus, pottery was required for storing food and water. Each culture's ceramics are characterized by unique styles and techniques, ranging from the Yangshao Culture of China to the Black- Figure Pottery of ancient Greece. Hence, ceramics are an integral part of our history.

Ceramics evolves as a culture carrier as well as a tool for daily life. All of them mark the period reflecting its population's customs, art, and innovations in society. From ancient Chinese celadon to the Japanese tea ceremony's tea bowls, ceramics have offered a passage from the past to our more recent history.

Currently, ceramics still holds the same position with numerous fans and followers. Its fabrication has now become a craft, which also is the symbol of culture and innovation. In this journey, we invite you to take an exploration of time. Initially, by discovering the roots of ceramics and subsequently the progress that has taken place throughout the ages. Finally, you will discover the immense possibilities still hidden in clay.

Traditional Chinese Ceramics

Colored Pottery From The Stone Age
The Blue Glaze Of The Song Dynasty

Chinese ceramics are very ancient, and the earliest record features the Yangshao Culture of the Neolithic Age. Yangshao pottery, characterized by red clay and colourful patterns, embodies natural beauty, which was a desire of early humans. The Tang Dynasty defined the Tang Sancai style with yellow, green, and white glaze, later on commonly used as burial objects to symbolize prosperity and cultural diversity during the period.

The Song Dynasty saw an exceptional rise in the popularity of celadon from this era. Its delicate surface and jade-like glaze, which were its key features, made it the most admired. Celadon fulfilled not only practical needs but also a spiritual role, and so, scholars and artists considered celadon an object of reverence.

Traditional Japanese Ceramics

Shigaraki Ware
Japanese Ceramic Tea Set

Japanese ceramics owes much to the beauty of tea culture. This feature embraces naturalism and rustic beauty. Apart from its functionality, tea ceremony utensils like tea bowls and teapots have an artistic value as well. Shigaraki Ware, an exclusive Japanese style, is also characterized by coarse texture and a natural glaze. It is commonly utilized in Japanese tea ceremonies, manifesting the philosophy of "wabi- sabi," which emphasizes imperfection. A striking feature of Arita Ware, so-called for its detailed paintings and white porcelain, is the masterful craftsmanship and the originality of Japanese ceramic art.

Traditional European Ceramics

Ancient Greek Black-Figure Pottery

European ceramics also boasts a rich history, with the Ancient Greek Black-Figure Pottery being a prime example. Mythical narratives are commonly found on this pottery, painted over red clay with black patterns on it. Its outline reflects the Greeks' fascination with their art and mythology. During the Renaissance period, Italian Majolica flourished as a symbol of ceramics' invaluable role in Europe, particularly noted for its bright colours and intensely detailed designs. The Wedgwood porcelain of 18th-century Britain, which embodied neoclassicism and the highest quality, was one of the most sought-after items by the European aristocracy.
